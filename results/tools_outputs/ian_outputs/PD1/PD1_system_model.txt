*   **TP63:** This transcription factor is involved in T cell differentiation and targets genes such as CD82, ITGB1, CDKN1A, MAF, SLC1A5, SMAD3, CDKN2C, and CDKN3. These targets are crucial for T cell function, cell cycle control, and signaling.
*   **FOXM1:** This transcription factor is a known regulator of cell cycle progression, with multiple targets within this pathway, including genes involved in DNA replication and mitosis.

*Mechanistic Model*
The identified relationships suggest a mechanistic model where upstream regulators, particularly transcription factors like RELA, SOX2, NR3C1, TP63, and FOXM1, orchestrate a coordinated response involving both immune activation and cell cycle progression in CD4⁺ PD-1⁺ T cells. RELA, through its broad targeting of immune-related genes (IFNG, ITGB1, VCAM1), likely drives inflammatory responses and cell adhesion. SOX2 and TP63 may influence T cell differentiation and the expression of adhesion molecules and cell cycle regulators. NR3C1, a known modulator of inflammation, also impacts immune cell trafficking and cytokine production. The prominent involvement of FOXM1 and other cell cycle genes (CCNA2, MYBL2, CDKN1A, etc.) suggests that activated T cells in this population are undergoing significant proliferation. This interplay could lead to an aberrant immune response characterized by excessive T cell activation and proliferation, contributing to the pathogenesis of rheumatoid arthritis.

*Hypothesis*
We hypothesize that in treatment-naïve early rheumatoid arthritis patients, CD4⁺ PD-1⁺ T cells exhibit an activated and proliferative phenotype driven by dysregulated upstream regulators, particularly RELA, SOX2, NR3C1, TP63, and FOXM1. These regulators likely promote a state of heightened immune responsiveness and uncontrolled cell cycling, contributing to the inflammatory milieu characteristic of early rheumatoid arthritis. The observed gene expression profile reflects a transition towards a more aggressive T cell phenotype, potentially involving enhanced effector functions and increased cellular turnover.
