Uveitis pathogenesis is characterized by a complex interplay of immune dysregulation, primarily driven by hyperactive complement system activation and altered adaptive and innate immune responses, leading to significant inflammation. This inflammatory milieu is further compounded by substantial extracellular matrix remodeling and altered cell adhesion dynamics, suggesting tissue damage and immune cell infiltration. The consistent enrichment of neuronal signaling pathways, particularly in peripheral blood transcriptomics, points to a potential, and possibly novel, direct link between systemic inflammation and neuronal function. Key transcription factors, including RELB, SMAD4, TFAP2C, and IRF1, are hypothesized to orchestrate these interconnected processes by regulating genes involved in inflammation, immune cell function, and tissue matrix alterations. The strong interactions among complement components, immune regulators like CD274, and ECM proteins like FN1 highlight a coordinated molecular network. The presence of Y-chromosome genes may indicate sex-specific immune responses contributing to disease heterogeneity.

**Hypothesis:** In uveitis, dysregulated immune responses, particularly complement activation and T cell costimulation, are central to disease pathogenesis. This is exacerbated by altered extracellular matrix remodeling and cell adhesion dynamics, potentially influenced by infectious triggers or autoantigens. These systemic changes may also manifest as altered neuronal signaling pathways, reflecting neuroinflammation or systemic effects on neuronal function. Upstream regulators like RELB, SMAD4, TFAP2C, and IRF1 orchestrate these interconnected processes, leading to chronic inflammation, tissue damage, and impaired immune regulation.
