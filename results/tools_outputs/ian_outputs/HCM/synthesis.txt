The integrated analysis of hypertrophic cardiomyopathy (HCM) heart tissue reveals a multi-faceted disease mechanism driven by a convergence of inflammation, immune dysregulation, metabolic reprogramming, and altered hemostasis, which collectively contribute to cardiac remodeling and dysfunction. Key transcription factors, particularly SMAD2/3, SPI1, and MAF, appear to orchestrate these changes by regulating a network of genes involved in cellular stress responses (GADD45A/B), immune cell activation (FCER1G, CD14), metabolic shifts towards glycolysis (LDHA), and coagulation (THBS1). Central hub proteins like THBS1, JAK2, and PLEK act as critical integrators, linking these diverse biological processes. The data strongly suggests that chronic inflammation and immune cell infiltration, potentially mediated by SPI1 and JAK2, trigger these metabolic shifts and coagulation abnormalities, ultimately leading to the cardiac fibrosis and hypertrophy characteristic of HCM.
